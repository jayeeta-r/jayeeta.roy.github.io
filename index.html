About

Jayeeta Roy is an interdisciplinary researcher with over twenty years of professional leadership experience, now based in Trollhattan, Sweden, where she is completing a second master's degree in Learning, Digitalisation and Sustainability (LeaDS) at Jonkoping University's School of Education and Communication. Her research focuses on the intersecting domains of sustainability, digital governance, social equity, and Work-Integrated Learning (WIL) — investigating how socio-technical systems and institutional structures shape the experiences and capacities of marginalised communities. Methodologically, she draws on mixed-methods research, SPSS-based statistical analysis, and NVivo-supported thematic coding, operating within theoretical frameworks that include Cultural-Historical Activity Theory (CHAT), Stakeholder Theory, and Transformative Leadership. She is actively seeking a doctoral position aligned with her interdisciplinary background, empirical track record, and commitment to practice-based, equity-centred scholarship.

Research

Jayeeta Roy's research agenda is organised around four substantive threads that are treated as analytically interdependent: developments in governance, technology, sustainability, and education operate on each other in ways that demand an integrated, cross-disciplinary approach. The threads below represent equal and mutually reinforcing lines of inquiry.

  • 01 — Research Interest
    Organisational Strategy and Leadership

    Scaling practice-based initiatives, transformative leadership, and navigating systemic contradictions during organisational or structural transitions. This thread draws on Activity Theory to map the contradictions that arise when organisations attempt to implement change from within inherited structures.

  • 02 — Research Interest
    Digitalisation, AI, and Society

    Socio-technical impacts of digital infrastructure and artificial intelligence on governance, education, and social inclusion. Particular attention is paid to the conditions under which AI-mediated systems expand or constrain access for structurally marginalised groups.

  • 03 — Research Interest
    Sustainability and Participatory Governance

    Socio-organisational approaches to sustainability transitions, stakeholder engagement, circular-economy models, and policy alignment. Empirically grounded in municipal-level governance, this thread examines how participatory processes can be designed to activate resident knowledge and agency.

  • 04 — Research Interest
    Inclusive Education and Social Justice

    Work-Integrated Learning (WIL), care-oriented practices, and inclusive policy frameworks for structurally marginalised populations. This thread draws on critical and decolonial perspectives to interrogate the assumptions embedded in mainstream educational policy and to foreground the methodological assets of non-traditional learners.

Research Statement

The central claim of my research is that sustainability, digital governance, social equity, and Work-Integrated Learning cannot be studied in productive isolation from one another in the 2020s. Each domain is constituted, in part, by the others: a municipal biodiversity strategy is simultaneously a digital-governance challenge, a question of whose knowledge counts, and a test of whether practice-based learning models can survive contact with institutional inertia. My work begins from that entanglement and attempts to hold it analytically without reducing any thread to a subordinate variable of the others.

The organising question running through my current and projected research is: how do socio-technical systems — digital infrastructure, artificial intelligence, governance regimes — reshape, and in turn get reshaped by, the everyday practices of marginalised learners and workers? This is not a question that resolves cleanly at either the macro or the micro level. It requires a methodology capable of moving between structural conditions and situated activity, which is why I work primarily in a mixed-methods mode, using Cultural-Historical Activity Theory (CHAT) and Stakeholder Theory as sensitising frameworks. CHAT in particular is generative for my purposes because it foregrounds contradictions — the gaps, tensions, and breakdowns within and between activity systems — as the productive sites where change either happens or stalls. Work-Integrated Learning functions in my research both as an empirical setting and as a pedagogical commitment: it insists that knowledge is produced in the encounter between formal academic preparation and real organisational practice, not before it.

The two master's theses I have completed serve as proof-of-concept for this approach. In Trollhattan, working within a municipality of more than 58,000 residents, I investigated how citizen-centred engagement strategies for biodiversity conservation could be designed when residents are understood not as recipients of policy but as active agents within an interconnected activity system of municipal planners, ecological scientists, and community members. In Karnataka, I examined an informal educational network supporting 632 displaced Rohingya children, where care-oriented learning and AI-mediated digital tools intersected in ways that both enabled and constrained educational sustainability. The two cases share a method — engaged, practice-based, contradiction-mapping — and a stance — care-oriented, equity-centred, attentive to what gets left out when institutions optimise for efficiency rather than inclusion.

The forward agenda for doctoral work extends this line of inquiry into a longer-term comparative programme. I am most interested in how WIL-informed approaches scale — or fail to scale — when digital infrastructure and AI tools are introduced into sustainability and inclusion work at the institutional level. The Swedish and Nordic context is significant here: high state capacity, strong civic-society traditions, and ambitious sustainability commitments coexist with structural inequities in educational access and persistent questions about how AI governance frameworks engage (or disengage) marginalised communities. This agenda maps naturally onto Nordic doctoral programmes in education, sustainability studies, and digital society, where the empirical and theoretical resources for this kind of comparative, cross-sectoral inquiry are most developed. My aim is to contribute, through a sustained programme of engaged scholarship, to the evidence base for how socio-technical transitions can be made more equitable — and to the methodological conversation about how to research such transitions honestly.

Teaching Philosophy

My teaching is organised around a care-oriented pedagogy that takes seriously the relational and affective dimensions of learning alongside its cognitive ones. Drawing on my WIL-informed practice, I approach the classroom — whether formal or informal — as a site where knowledge is made in dialogue between academic frameworks and the lived expertise that every student brings from their own history of work, community, and struggle. This means holding space for contradiction and uncertainty rather than resolving them prematurely, and it means designing learning activities that are anchored in real problems rather than sanitised illustrations of theory. Critical and decolonial perspectives have fundamentally shaped how I understand knowledge production itself: the question of who gets to be cited, whose categories organise a field, and whose experience counts as data is not incidental to research education but central to it. Since 2011, I have mentored first-generation university students through Udayan Care's Corporate Readiness Programme, and what that experience has taught me above all is that non-traditional learners bring methodological and epistemological assets — skills of translation, code-switching, reading systems under pressure — that mainstream academia persistently under-uses. Good teaching, as I understand it, is precisely the practice of making those assets visible and academically legible.
